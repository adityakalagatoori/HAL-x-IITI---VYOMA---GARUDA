Overview

GARUDA is a hybrid physics + machine-learning digital twin that estimates the health of a four-stage turbojet's compressor, combustor, and turbine directly from gas-path sensor readings, predicts engine performance (thrust and fuel consumption), detects real and sensor-originated faults, and provides maintenance recommendations — all through an interactive dashboard including a live 3D engine model.

Rather than treating this as a pure prediction problem, GARUDA is built around a discovered fact about the provided dataset: raw sensor readings alone explain under 16% of true component health, dominated by flight-condition noise. A physics layer is therefore not an optional add-on here — it is the mechanism that makes health estimation possible at all.

System Architecture

Physics Layer

0-D Brayton-cycle gas-path inversion — recovers compressor and turbine isentropic efficiency and combustor pressure retention directly from sensor readings. No training required.

Sensor Integrity Layer

Hard physical rules (verified 100% true on real data) plus a calibrated plausibility envelope, distinguishing a lying sensor from a genuinely degrading component before either reaches the health estimate.

Health Estimation — Two Modes

Flight-Log Match: closed-form exact reconstruction for a known engine. Fleet Baseline Estimate: hierarchical trend + monotonicity-constrained gradient boosting + conformal uncertainty, for a brand-new engine with no history.

Decision Layer

Remaining useful life projection, counterfactual "what-if-restored" simulation, ranked maintenance recommendations, and a mission-impact estimate.

Methodology

Physics Equations

Compressor isentropic efficiency: η_c = (T2s − T01) / (T2 − T01), and turbine isentropic efficiency: η_t = (T3 − T4) / (T3 − T4s), computed from ram-corrected stagnation conditions at the inlet. Combustor performance is characterized via pressure retention (P3/P2).

Model Selection

Each model choice was tested, not assumed. For exact reconstruction, a closed-form alternating least squares solution was compared directly against a neural alternative (a PCA context encoder feeding a diagnostic head): the neural approach scored R²=−0.37 (worse than predicting the mean); the closed-form approach scored R²=1.0. With only 10 engines in the provided dataset, there is not enough data for a network to learn a useful prior — the simpler architecture was chosen because the data justified it, not by default.

Uncertainty Quantification

Split conformal prediction is used for the Fleet Baseline Estimate mode, providing a distribution-free coverage guarantee rather than a bootstrap approximation.

Key Differentiators

  • Physics-informed, not black-box. Every health estimate is grounded in thermodynamic equations, not purely learned correlations.
  • Sensor Integrity Check. Distinguishes a malfunctioning sensor from a genuinely degrading component — a capability absent from standard health-monitoring approaches in the reviewed literature.
  • Tested, not assumed, architecture. The neural-vs-closed-form ablation study is a direct, quantified comparison, not a design assumption.
  • Exact discovered structure. The dataset's underlying degradation law (a single normalized curve shared across all engines) and its overall-health composition formula were independently discovered through analysis, not provided or assumed.
  • Dual-mode design. Exact reconstruction where possible, generalizable estimation where necessary — covering both a known engine and a completely new one.

Known Limitations

Conformal prediction coverage. In back-testing, observed coverage on the Fleet Baseline Estimate ranged 83–93% against a 90% nominal target — a known finite-sample effect of calibrating on a small dataset, disclosed rather than hidden.
Flight-Log Match requires history. The exact-reconstruction mode needs at least two recorded cycles of an engine's own history; it has no defined answer for a completely new engine with zero prior data — which is exactly the case the Fleet Baseline Estimate mode is designed to cover instead.
Mission-impact estimate. Raw fuel-consumption readings in the provided dataset are dominated by instantaneous flight condition rather than health, so the mission fuel-impact figure is derived from the validated health index via a documented engineering approximation rather than a directly fitted model.
