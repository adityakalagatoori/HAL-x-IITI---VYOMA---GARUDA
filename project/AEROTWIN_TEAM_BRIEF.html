1. One-line product summary

AeroTwin is a hybrid physics + machine-learning digital twin that estimates the health of a four-stage turbojet's compressor, combustor, and turbine from gas-path sensor readings, predicts performance (thrust/fuel consumption), detects faults in real time, and recommends maintenance — presented through an interactive dashboard with a live 3D engine model.

Positioning: most competing teams will submit a black-box ML model on the C-MAPSS-style dataset. We differentiate by building an actual physics layer (a 0-D thermodynamic gas-path solver), which is what makes health estimates interpretable, enables real anomaly detection, and let us discover the dataset's exact underlying structure.

2. System architecture

Stage A — Physics Layer

0-D Brayton-cycle gas-path inversion (compressor/turbine isentropic efficiency, combustor pressure retention) computed per-cycle from sensor readings. No training required — closed-form thermodynamics.

Stage B — Two Prediction Modes

Flight-Log Match: exact per-engine reconstruction (discovered deterministic degradation law). Fleet Baseline Estimate: hierarchical population+engine trend, monotonic-constrained gradient boosting residual, conformal uncertainty — generalizes to a brand-new engine.

Stage C — Decision Support

Verdict/flight-readiness, RUL projection, counterfactual "what-if-fixed" simulator, ranked maintenance recommendations, mission analysis simulator, templated Q&A.

Presentation Layer

Single self-contained interactive dashboard (charts, 3D engine model, sliders) — no backend server required, fully offline-capable, security-reviewed.

5. Key findings from our own EDA (worth highlighting — most teams won't find these)

  • Health is deterministic, not noisy: every engine's health trajectory follows an identical normalized shape curve (cross-engine std ≈ 1e-15) — only two free parameters per engine (initial health, total drop) fully determine its trajectory.
  • Raw sensors are dominated by flight-condition noise, not health: raw sensor ratios correlate <0.15 with health; Cycle number alone correlates ~0.90–0.97. This is why a naive black-box model would quietly lean on Cycle without understanding why.
  • OverallHealth has an exact closed form: 0.4·Compressor + 0.3·Combustor + 0.3·Turbine, R²=1.0 — not approximated, discovered by regression.
  • Thrust/TSFC are flight-condition-driven, not health-driven: confirmed via correlation analysis (TSFC correlates 0.80–0.95 with fuel flow/T3/T4, ~0 with health) — this is why our Mission Impact panel uses health as the basis instead of raw TSFC (see §6).
  • Subsystems degrade independently, not causally: cross-subsystem decline-rate correlation is weak (0.05, 0.31, −0.06) — no evidence one subsystem's wear drives another's.

6. Honest disclosures & limitations (know these before the jury Q&A does)

Mission fuel-penalty metric was originally wrong — a first version showed some engines "improving" fuel efficiency while degrading (physically nonsensical), traced to raw TSFC being flight-condition-noise-dominated. Fixed by switching to a clearly labeled engineering approximation based on the validated health index instead of the noisy raw signal.
Conformal prediction undershoots its nominal coverage: 83–93% observed vs. 90% target, a known finite-sample effect of calibrating on ~50 points. Disclosed in the dashboard's Methodology panel rather than hidden.
Payload/Weather fields in Mission Analysis are illustrative, not model-fitted — the dataset has no payload or weather columns. Clearly tagged "Illustrative" in the UI rather than presented as learned.
Neural context-encoder underperforms closed-form at this data scale (only 10 engines) — reported as a deliberate engineering decision (matching model complexity to data size), not hidden or spun as a win for the neural approach.
3D engine model is procedural geometry, not a Fusion 360 export — no CAD model exists yet. Framed honestly in the dashboard copy and should be framed the same way in the PPT: the interaction architecture (click, color, physics-binding) is built and works; a nicer mesh is a drop-in swap, not unbuilt functionality.
